In this section, you will explore some of the most compelling examples of the horror genre. With a selection of films, you will see how horror cinema evokes adrenaline and curiosity. Each film promises thrilling moments and introduces the fundamental elements of the horror genre. Are you ready to embark on this dark journey?

The Others (2001)


Director: Alejandro Amenábar

Cast: Nicole Kidman, Fionnula Flanagan, Christopher Eccleston

"The Others" takes the audience to a secluded mansion where a mother, awaiting her husband's return from war, lives with her two children who are sensitive to light. The mansion, where Grace, played by Nicole Kidman, strictly educates her children in religious matters, becomes the scene of mysterious events alongside unexpected guests. The film breathes new life into classic ghost stories and presents an atmosphere that keeps the audience in suspense until the end. With a tight screenplay and powerful performances, "The Others" stands out as one of the prominent films in the horror genre.

Exorcist (1973)


Director: Willia Friedkin

Cast: Ellen Burstyn, Max von Sydow, Linda Blair

Released in 1973 and adapted from William Peter Blatty's novel of the same name, "The Exorcist" is considered one of the most famous and influential horror films of all time. The film revolves around the horrifying events that unfold when a young girl (Linda Blair) becomes possessed by a demon. Her mother (Ellen Burstyn) and two priests risk their lives to save her from this evil entity. With realistic makeup effects, unforgettable music, and a gripping atmosphere, "The Exorcist" offers a profoundly impactful experience for its viewers. This film, which sparked significant controversy at the time of its release, has become a landmark in horror cinema.

Audition (1999)


Director: Takashi Miike

Cast: Ryo Ishibashi, Eihi Shiina, Tetsu Sawaki

For its first 80 minutes or so, this muted J-horror classic plays like a sweet little love story between Shigeharu, a gentle widower, and Asami, a fragile ex-ballerina — two wounded souls who've miraculously found each other and may just live happily ever after. Then a lumpy sack mysteriously moves and that last half hour happens…and holy hell, does it happen. Prodigious genre maestro Takashi Miike effectively locates the pressure points and most sensitive parts of our unconscious and then proceeds to gleefully pierce into them "deeper and deeper," as Asami whispers. Even with the wire-administered amputations and decidedly non-therapeutic acupuncture, the sadism of Audition feels almost self-administered — an extreme manifestation of the power play between men and women. Brace yourself.
